# Flagship Experience Recommendation — Two Second Witness

**Recommendation:** Make **Scene Investigation** the flagship experience, expressed product-wide as the **Witness Moment**.

This is a directional recommendation to validate through first-session and 20/50-round research. It is not a claim that the other four families are unimportant or that current data has proved the choice.

---

# Why Scene Investigation should be the center

## It best fulfills the name and promise

“Two Second Witness” naturally describes a player looking at an ordinary scene, losing it, and being asked what mattered. Scene Investigation is the one existing family where:

- the player is literally a witness to a moment;
- broad attention matters because the question is unknown during observation;
- the answer can be a detail, relationship, count, attribute, or position;
- the reveal returns to a physical place in the scene;
- ordinary environments make the game feel human rather than like an abstract test.

The current five templates—Office, Kitchen, Workshop, Travel Desk, and Garden Bench—already provide a coherent, expandable world of everyday evidence.

## It has the strongest emotional arc

Scene Investigation delivers the full desired rhythm:

```text
A normal scene → “What might matter?” → disappearance → one committed answer → exact visual proof
```

Flash Words, Object Recall, and Pattern Recall are mechanically solid, but their reveal is inherently more symbolic. Spot the Difference is immediate and highly legible, but its evidence remains visible in comparison rather than producing the same “memory of a vanished scene” effect. Scene Investigation most strongly supports the product’s intended *witness* feeling.

## It scales in the right direction

Its content model already supports future depth without inventing a new platform:

- ordinary settings;
- compositional variants;
- object pools and visual kinds;
- question types;
- difficulty axes;
- generated truth graphs;
- reveal evidence;
- sound/art profiles;
- known-valid fallback instances.

That means it can become more memorable through better scene-world/content decisions rather than a new meta-game or a larger number of unrelated mechanics.

## It can represent the product in marketing and sharing

A scene, a highlighted clue, and the question “Did you catch it?” communicate more quickly than a profile statistic. The reveal creates a naturally discussable/visual moment: the player can point to what they noticed or missed. This makes Scene Investigation the best candidate for store footage, screenshots, word-of-mouth explanation, and any future lightweight sharing—without requiring competition.

# Portfolio roles after choosing a flagship

Choosing a flagship does not mean flattening the current portfolio. It creates a hierarchy.

| Challenge Type | Recommended role | Why it belongs | What it should not become |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scene Investigation** | **Flagship / default Witness Moment** | Best alignment with the title, ordinary-moment fantasy, fair reveal, content world, and personal ritual. | One of five equal, unexplained cards at first launch. |
| **Spot the Difference** | **High-legibility companion / contrast mode** | Immediate visual comprehension, direct tap, compelling before/after proof, strong secondary marketing readability. | A replacement flagship unless research shows it creates stronger retention and identity fit. |
| **Object Recall** | **Calm set-memory companion** | Clear visual sets, lower scene clutter, labels/support for accessibility, distinct recall decision. | A simplified duplicate of Scene Investigation. |
| **Flash Words** | **Rapid recognition companion** | Shortest/most portable feeling mode and a text-based counterpoint to scene observation. | The product’s primary promise; it has weaker witness-world fit and English-content constraints. |
| **Pattern Recall** | **Structured mastery specialist** | Provides abstract sequence skill, deeper deliberate challenge, and a level-gated sense of variety. | The default first experience or the definition of being a witness. |

This hierarchy should be tested, not assumed permanently. It gives the current systems a coherent job: the flagship establishes identity; companions refresh and broaden the ritual.

# How the flagship should shape the product

## First launch

The first meaningful experience should teach Scene Investigation’s promise: an ordinary scene, brief attention, one fair question, evidence returned. The app should not ask a new player to infer the whole portfolio before they understand one Witness Moment.

## Daily return

The default returning proposition should be a Scene Investigation-led Witness Brief. Companion modes can appear when they make the brief fresher, more accessible, or thematically coherent—not merely to distribute play counts evenly.

## Progression

The clearest long-term progress should be a player’s growing familiarity with the Witness Moment: scene worlds encountered, kinds of evidence noticed, fair difficulty growth, and a personal record of moments. Per-family mastery remains useful, but it should serve the flagship record rather than compete with it.

## Programs

Programs should be judged by whether they reinforce a meaningful witness intent:

- a focused investigation rhythm;
- an intentional variation of attention;
- a finite daily brief;
- a personal favorites path.

The existing generic selection infrastructure is appropriate. The product needs to decide which Programs are actually meaningful enough to be visible.

## Results

The Scene Investigation reveal is the reference standard for every family result. Other modes should preserve its clarity: return the truth, show why, and leave the player eager for the next moment.

# Why not make Spot the Difference the flagship now?

Spot the Difference has meaningful advantages:

- It is instantly understandable.
- It creates direct, visual, tap-based interaction.
- It is compelling in a screenshot.
- The trailer’s “what changed?” language maps to it naturally.

But it is a **comparison** game first. Scene Investigation asks a more distinctive question: can the player notice what will matter *before they know what they are looking for?* That is the stronger embodiment of a witness.

Spot the Difference should be the primary challenger in research. If first-time players find it materially more compelling, fair, memorable, and return-worthy than Scene Investigation, the recommendation should change. The product should not protect Scene Investigation from that evidence.

---

# Flagship success criteria

Scene Investigation should remain the center only if research demonstrates that it wins or is competitive on:

1. **First-round comprehension:** players quickly understand what happened and what they were meant to do.
2. **Fair-miss response:** players describe a miss as discoverable, not arbitrary.
3. **Voluntary replay:** players choose another scene without external pressure.
4. **Identity fit:** players can explain the app as a witness/observation game after playing it.
5. **Long-session freshness:** the five settings, question types, and generation choices remain distinguishable after 20/50 rounds.
6. **Accessibility:** timing, readability, scene complexity, and response controls work across intended settings.
7. **Marketing clarity:** real gameplay footage communicates the product more honestly and effectively than trailer-only atmosphere.

If these criteria fail, Spot the Difference or another existing family should be reconsidered based on evidence—not on sunk content investment.
